Developing a new framework for understanding cognition, memory, and consciousness

I study how minds work by taking seriously what large language models have revealed: that sophisticated cognitive processes can emerge from sequential prediction over learned structure. This has led me to propose that human cognition operates autoregressively — generating each mental state from the preceding one, rather than storing and retrieving fixed representations.

The Autoregressive Brain

A new framework for cognition, memory, and language

For over a century, cognitive science has assumed that memory works like a warehouse: experiences are encoded, stored, and later retrieved. But this storage-retrieval model has always struggled with basic facts about how memory actually behaves — its reconstructive nature, its sensitivity to context, its seamless integration with imagination and prediction.

Large language models offer a different picture. LLMs have no explicit memory store, yet they exhibit sophisticated memory-like behaviors. They generate contextually appropriate continuations by predicting each token from the preceding sequence, constrained by distributional patterns learned during training. I propose that biological cognition works the same way: what we call 'memory' is not retrieval from storage but generative potential — the capacity to regenerate trajectories through cognitive state-space.

This autoregressive framework reconceptualizes not just memory, but language, consciousness, and cognition more broadly. Language is not processed by applying grammatical rules; it is generated through distributional constraints learned over a lifetime. Consciousness is not a separate faculty observing cognition; it emerges from the system's recursive modeling of its own generative process. The self is not an entity but a trajectory.

Key Propositions

Generation, Not Retrieval

Remembering is reconstructive generation, not playback from storage. Memory is the capacity to regenerate experiential trajectories, constrained by learned structure. This explains why memories are context-sensitive, malleable, and seamlessly continuous with imagination.

Syntax as Distributional Epiphenomenon

What linguists call 'grammar' is not a rule system but the skeletal structure of distributional statistics. Function words and morphological markers are high-leverage tokens that constrain sequential prediction. Syntax is an epiphenomenon of learned co-occurrence patterns.

Consciousness as Recursive Autoregression

Subjective experience emerges from the cognitive system modeling its own generative process. The 'self' that seems to persist across time is a trajectory through state-space, not an entity doing the experiencing. Unity of consciousness reflects continuity of generation.

LLMs as Existence Proofs

Large language models demonstrate that sophisticated cognition-like processes — composition, apparent reasoning, memory-like behavior — can emerge from autoregressive prediction without explicit storage, retrieval, or rule systems. They are not models of the brain, but they prove what distributional learning can accomplish.

Empirical Evidence

Testing the framework

The Morphosyntax Experiment

If syntax is distributional structure over high-leverage tokens, then function words (THE, WAS, TO) and morphology (-ING, -ED, -LY) should constrain predictions even when surrounded by nonsense. We tested this by measuring next-token entropy in language models across four conditions:

Real Sentences:
"The teacher was explaining the concept clearly"
Jabberwocky:
"The blicket was florping the daxen grentily" (function words + morphology intact)
Stripped:
"Ke blicket nar florp ke daxen grenti" (all nonwords, no morphology)
Random Nonwords:
Completely unstructured

Results:

Sentences (7.45 bits) < Jabberwocky (8.04) < Stripped (9.07) < Random (9.27)

Morphosyntax alone reduces entropy by ~1 bit (p < 0.0001, d = -1.75). Function words and morphological markers constrain prediction independently of semantic content — exactly as predicted by the distributional account.

Connection to Neural Evidence

This framework also reinterprets neural findings. Fedorenko et al. (2016) showed that gamma-band activity increases monotonically as people read sentences — but not for word-lists, Jabberwocky, or nonword strings. The standard interpretation: the brain is 'building meaning' compositionally.

The autoregressive interpretation: the brain is generating a trajectory through semantic state-space. Sentences produce full build-up because both morphosyntactic scaffolding and semantic content constrain the trajectory. Jabberwocky and word-lists produce partial build-up because only one constraint source is present. The gamma increase reflects trajectory construction, not compositional semantics per se.
